Let's learn about water scarcity

This video is a simple explanation of what water scarcity is, and how it affects us. While it is shallow in its information, it covers a lot of ground about the pressing issue.

Why is this important?

We must educate ourselves about what water crises are, and what it can do to our lives. But further down the line, it is not only our lives that are at stake. Our children, their children. The animals that we share this planet with. All of us will face the aftermath of overconsumption of water. And for the same reason we teach our children about major historical events, in some ways to prevent them from happening again, we must learn about water to be able to forward that information.

The major reasons for water scarcity as mentioned in Why is it Happening Page are pollution, population, agriculture, and global climate change. To combat these effects, we look towards big organizations to make the moves. And one of the biggest non-political organizations in this is the World Wildlife Foundation2. While their name might tell that they work for wildlife only, the water issues are a major concern for both humanities such as animals on earth.

One international treaty was established to protect wetlands around the globe, the "Ramsar Convention3". The treaty was established to hold the country where these wetlands are located accountable and committed to protecting those wetlands. One example is Sweden, where I am from. Sweden entered the treaty on April 5th, 1975, and currently 68 wetlands4.UK4 and Mexico4 trump Sweden with quite a margin. the UK has almost 1.3 million hectares of important wetlands, while Mexico has a mindblowing 8.6million hectares. Between those two countries, Mexico has a much larger problem with water and its sustainability but has six times as many wetlands of international importance according to the Ramsar report. Overpopulation, pollution, and climate change have hit Mexico harder than the UK. This is the message I want to convey, that the amount of wetlands does not equal usable water.

Water Facts

Trying to measure individual's water consumption is hard, but with help from another major organization, namely the Water Footprint Network5. This will go into much more detailed and nuanced information. The WFN, together with a large network of organizations and professionals are concerned with the increasing water scarcity and pollution on our planet. I want to give you a few interesting facts about water consumption we usually might not think about. But first I want to quote an article from 2010 that was written by UNESCO-IHE, Institute for Water Education:

"We follow the water footprint definitions and methodology as set out in Hoekstra et al. (2009). The blue water footprint refers to consumption of blue water resources (surface and groundwater) along the supply chain of a product. ‘Consumption’ refers to loss of water from the available ground-surface water body in a catchment area. Losses occur when water evaporates, returns to another catchment area or the sea or is incorporated into a product. The green water footprint refers to consumption of green water resources (rainwater in so far as it does not become run-off). The grey water footprint refers to pollution and is defined as the volume of freshwater that is required to assimilate the load of pollutants given natural background concentrations and existing ambient water quality standards. National Water Footprint Accounts, May 2011(6)"

From this, hard-to-read quote we take away that, the WaterFootprintNetwork uses three different labels on what they use to measure water consumption. Let me explain them more in detail.

The Green Water Footprint is measuring water from precipitation that is stored in the root zone of the soil and evaporated, transpired or incorporated by plants. It is particularly relevant for agricultural, horicultural and forestry producs.

The Blue Water Footprint is water that has been sourced from surface or groundwater resources and is either evaporated, incorporated into a product or taken from one body of water and returned to another, or returned at a different time. Irrigated agriculture, industry and domestic water use can each have a blue water footprint.

The Gray Water Footprint is the amount of fresh water required to assimilate pollutants to meet specific water quality standards. The grey water footprint considers point-source pollution discharged to a freshwater resource directly through a pipe or indirectly through runoff or leaching from the soil, impervious surfaces, or other diffuse sources

Now we know about the footprints, and what they mean. Now we can get to the facts. An intimidating fact about water footprints is how large the numbers can become. The global water footprint of humanity during the span of 1996-2005 was 9087Bn cubic meters per year. (74% green, 11% blue, 15% grey). Agricultural production contributes 92% to this total footprint.

On a smaller scale, we can compare the water footprint of a soy burger produced in the Netherlands. That water footprint is roughly 160 liters. While a beef burger produced in the same country costs on avarage 1000 liters. Or the fact that Japan with an average footprint of 1380 cubic meters per year and capita. And that 77% of that has its water footprint outside the country border. The United States receives a much more concerning verdict. With a yearly consumption of 2840 cubic meters per year and capita, and 20% of that footprint is external. What this means is that the citizens of the US are consuming water on other country's behalf, the largest external footprint this consumption makes is in the Yangtze River Basin, China. This River has its own issues. according to a Dutch research foundation, the Yangtze river together with 9 other rivers transports 90% of all plastics that reaches the ocean.7 Exporting that drinking water is not one of the problems it seems.
