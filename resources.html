A Global Crisis

Water is a dwindling resource all over the world. While villages with a smaller population and economical strength is suffering more than large urban civilizations, there is no reason to not think that the issues stop there. For a very clear depiction of what is going on Head over to the Water Scarcity Clock.

What Is Happening?

In 2000, almost 1.7bn people were living in water scarce areas. As of right now (2020), that number has skyrocketed to 2.3bn. And in 2030, it is expected to be 2.7bn.

Water scarcity is a growing issue that is rapidly increasing on every continent on earth, but some locations are far more exposed to this issue. We see these issues growing much faster in countries closer to the equator, increased temperatures and extensive periods of drought contributes to the matter. A growing number of regions are reporting that they are reaching the limits for sustainable delivery of water. Water could be scarce due to physical shortage, mostly refered to as "Physical Water Scarcity". Physical scarcity is a result of a population that grows immensly every day. Water scarcity can also be caused by a lack of infrastructure, or a constitutional neglect that results in an inability to ensure a regular supply, this is known as "Economical Water Scarcity". Many regions that suffer from the "Economical" version of water scarcity is rapidly growing urban areas where neighbouring water resources are put under heavy pressure. The crisis is not a global event, as of right now. But individual countries are suffering greatly, and need to tackle the problems that the water scarcity puts on the regions. Changes in how available water is to the public will also heavely affect general health and reduce food quality. According to GWI, roughly 700 million people all over the world may be displaced due to water scarcity by 2030.

Statistics show that the worlds water use has grown at twice the rate of the population increase over the world. The fact that over 2 billion people today live in constant water stress today, and with increasing heavy load on our environment makes us and our partners believe that these numbers only will rise in the future. In many countries around the world, women and girls have the primary responsibility to manage the household water supply. Firstly, this is in many cases a time-consuming and heavy duty, in stead of working or aquiring education. Secondly, without freshwater that is safe to consume and to maintain adequate hygine at home, the rate of pregnancy-failure will increase, alongside water carried diseases. Even tho we dont have extensive coverage of the COVID-19 spread in some of these zones that's heavily affected by water scarcity, the hygiene issue it creates would make the containment of the virus harder to maintain.

Why is this happening?

There are multiple reasons and then some to why water is becoming a scarce resource on our planet. Our earth contains an immense ammount of water, however, the oceans are to the 99nth percentile consisting of saltwater, makingit it undrinkable. And the vast majority of fresh water is locked inside glaciers, making water a much more rare resource then one might think.

Water being scarce across the global has already proven to create political instability and even trigger refugee dynamics. With temperatures rising to more extreme levels, weather becomes less predictable, an exaple is many of the countries that relies on heavy rainfall, snowmelt, groundwater or rivers may expect severe decline in water quality due to the rising temperatures.

Overexploitation of natural resources is a major jeopardization on our natural ecosystems in lakes, rivers and other aquatic sectors. This will and have had dire consequences on the reliability and sustainablity of water supplies in adjecent areas. As mentioned above, the "Economical Water Scarcity" may afflict politicial standings in countries that share water resources. Imagine if you are living on one side of a big fresh water river, and recently, the country on the other side built a big factory needing water to cool their turbines, as well as the pollution eventually rendering the water unusable for individuals. Once the entire lake is depleted, the risk now is that saltwater intrusion from coastal areas bringing in toxins as arsenic and fluoride, killing the nearby crops. While the conflicts play a part in the issue, according to UNECE only 37 incidents of acute conflict over water areas have been reported since 1948, and almost 300 agreements were negotiated and signed to cover these waters.

Finances is a problem here. The UN SDG-6 wich is to reach everyone with "W.A.S.H" (water, sanitation, and hygiene) by 2030 is ambitious, however, this is a crucial goal to eradicate extreme poverty. But 80% of the countries that signed on for the SDG6 agreement reports insufficient financing towards the goal. The main source of finance towards this project comes from taxing individuals and businesses, tariffs paid by households and governments, and the provision of goodwill. And there are significant gaps in between actually financing, governance and policies and the aspiration to make W.A.S.H's reach global reality. It is projected that even without considering operational and maintenance costs, the financial aid needs to be tripled to achive the goal.

What can we do?

3.4 million people die each year from scarce and contaminated water source. As mentioned above, many millions of children and women spend up to six hours every day collecting water, from sources that is located far away and in many cases polluted. Those six hours are time spent not generating income, tending to family members or going to school. And at any given moment, almost half of every hospital bed is occupied by a patient thats suffering from diseases directly connected to the lack of acces to clean water.

To work against this crisis is a global undertaking. Different parts of the planet consumes different ammounts of water. And some targets must be more prioritized than other. The infrastructure of water transportation and conservation must be improved, as these are key components towards a sustainable life and accomplising the SDG-6 goals. The farming and aggriculture secture consumes much more water than any other sector on earth, and examples of clean technology that does not require water to run is being used to reduce the ammount of water needed to sustain the farms. Technology is greatly improving our lifes and also how we handle our water in new ways. Solar desalination and smart irrigation is only two of many new savy technologies that keeps over consumption of water to a minimum. Read more about what organisations are doing on our partners page

Learn how to recycle wastewater will become a much larger part of our lifes in the near future, no, not not showering, or not flushing the toilets. But harvesting rainwater and allowing groundwater to naturally recharge. This is called, 'Groundwater Recharge' allowing water on the surface to the earth to eventually return to groundwater, a century old process to prevent scarcity. With the lack of sanitation and proper sewage the water will be unsafe to drink, because it will be contaminated with noumerus diseases. Monitoring and measuring the quality of the waters around the globe is paramount to maintaining water that is clean enough to consume, where ever in the world we are. This is one, if not the biggest issue to making the scarcity prevention a global movement. Many places that is affected the hardest is small villages or places that are hard to reach on foot, nevertheless with heavy equipment to measure the cleanliness of water. Many organisations today have developed smaller devices to measure the quality of the water, with nothing more than a slightly large siringe. While this does nothing to handle the level of quality in the waters, it gives scientists and researchers an idea of what and where things are happening.

All of these things may seem hard to contribute to, and they are, unless you are a scientist to help develop products, or wealthy enough to donate substantial ammounts of money. But the most important thing, that no money can buy, is to learn, to be aware and educate ourselves about the issue. To eradicate water scarcity from our future on this planet, it is of critical value that we reform all forms of consumption, from individual use and large corporations. And if we are educated about the matter we will be able to handle it better. There are hundreds of resources that helps education people daily, read more about those on our Resources page
